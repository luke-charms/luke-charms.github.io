Bisimilarity

Theorem: The union of two bisimulation relations R1,R2 on an LTS L is a bisimulation relation on L

Theorem: For any given LTS L there is a largest bisimulation on L

  • We write ~ to denote the largest bisimulation on an LTS - we call this relation bisimilarity
  • Similar to similarity, bisimilarity is a coinductively defined relation that comes along with a coinductive proof technique

Coinduction for Bisimilarity

The Bisimulation Game

Rules of the Game:

The game starts at position (x,y) and you are, once again playing against a Demon opponent

  1. The demon first picks where to play, either from x or from y
  1. The demon then picks a move from their chosen start state
  1. You must start from the other start state and choose a matching move
  1. The game goes back to Step 1, changing the position to (x‚Äô,y‚Äô) where (x',y') are the states reached by both demon and player

If at any point a player cannot make a move, that player loses

If the game goes on forever, you win

(The demon always gets to choose whether to play in the "x" states or the "y" states when making its next move)

Winning Strategies

Theorem: for any two states, x ~ y if and only if the non-demon player has a winning strategy in the bisimulation game

  • This gives us a proof technique to show that two states are not in the bisimilarity relation - i.e. demon has a winning strategy from (x,y) means x ‚àºÃ∏ y

Corollary bisimilarity implies simulation equivalence

Proof:

  • We can prove the contrapositive of this statement (assume that x is not simulation equivalent to y) which then proves that x is not bisimilar to y
  • We know, by assumption, that demon has a winning strategy in the simulation game starting from (x,y)
  • This can be turned in to a strategy in the bisimulation game for demon by always choosing the "x" state as its starting state
  • This strategy is a winning strategy in the bisimulation game also so x is not bisimilar to y.
